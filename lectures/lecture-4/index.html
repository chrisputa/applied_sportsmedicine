ACL Rehab Protocols Insights

June 26, 2026

Early phase 0–6 weeks

Early rehabilitation focuses on safe graft protection while restoring motion, reducing effusion and pain, and re‑establishing quadriceps activation to enable later strengthening and gait retraining. Protocols endorse early weight bearing and a combination of weight‑bearing and non‑weight‑bearing exercises with adjunct modalities used only to facilitate exercise participation when needed.

  • Goals and targets

    • Primary goals Restore pain‑free range of motion, control swelling, re‑establish quadriceps activation, and normalize gait early [1] [2].
    • Timing guidance Early weight bearing and accelerated approaches (without routine postoperative bracing) have been shown feasible and commonly recommended in evidence‑based protocols [1] [3].

  • Typical exercises and modalities

    • ROM and mobility Heel slides, passive/assisted knee flexion and extension work, patellar mobilizations when indicated [1] [2].
    • Quadriceps activation Isometrics, straight‑leg raises, and neuromuscular electrical stimulation as adjunct when quadriceps inhibition is present [2] [3].
    • Early loading Low‑load closed‑chain tasks (mini‑squats, weight shifts) progressing as swelling and pain allow [1] [2].
    • Adjuncts Cryotherapy, compression and short‑term modalities may permit earlier, pain‑free exercise but evidence for dose–response is limited [3].

  • Contraindications and precautions

    • Protect healing Progress exercise intensity guided by pain, effusion, and movement quality rather than calendar alone [1] [3].

Mid phase 6–16 weeks

The mid phase prioritizes progressive strength (especially quadriceps and hip) and neuromuscular control to restore single‑limb function and prepare for running and higher‑level tasks. Progression should be criterion driven, integrating targeted neuromuscular training to address deficits in strength, power and movement quality.

  • Strength and neuromuscular emphases

    • Progressive resistance Systematic increase in loading through bilateral then unilateral resistance exercises (leg press, squats, lunges) and targeted hip strengthening [4] [5].
    • Neuromuscular training Balance, single‑leg stability, perturbation training, and movement technique drills to reduce aberrant movement patterns and limb‑dominance deficits [4] [6].
    • Functional progression Begin running return protocols only after meeting objective strength, ROM and gait criteria; introduce low‑level plyometrics and hopping drills later in this window when criteria are met [5] [4].

  • Representative exercises and progressions

    • Foundational Two‑leg squats, hip abduction/resisted band work, Romanian deadlifts for posterior chain.
    • Transitional Step‑downs, single‑leg squats, forward and lateral hops with emphasis on quality.
    • Criterion evidence Neuromuscular programs added to standard strength work improved outcomes and may reduce second‑injury risk versus strength‑only approaches [6].

Late phase 16 plus weeks

Late phase rehabilitation targets sport‑specific capacity: high‑velocity strength, power, multidirectional plyometrics and progressive return to cutting, pivoting and competition under load. Advancement to unrestricted sport should be based on meeting objective return‑to‑sport criteria rather than fixed time alone, and many guidelines recommend extended rehabilitation up to 9–12 months for safer reintegration.

    • Return‑to‑sport test battery Incorporate quantitative strength testing, hop tests, movement quality assessment and patient‑reported outcomes; limb symmetry indices near 90% are commonly used thresholds in criteria‑driven models [4] [7].

Progression criteria and considerations

Progression should be milestone and criterion based, adapted for graft biology and individual factors, with attention to exercise selection (open vs closed chain), common pitfalls and emerging adjuncts. Several contemporary guidelines and trials support objective testing, delayed unrestricted return, and incorporation of secondary‑prevention programs.

  • Milestone and criterion thresholds

    • Strength symmetry Many criterion‑based frameworks use a quadriceps limb symmetry index of ~90% as a clearance benchmark for late‑stage tasks and return‑to‑sport testing [4] [7].
    • Test battery Recommended components include isokinetic or manual dynamometry strength, hop tests, movement quality assessments, and patient‑reported outcome measures to guide progression [7] [4].
    • Timing guidance Rehabilitation commonly extends to 9–12 months before unrestricted sport, with earlier return linked to higher re‑injury risk in some cohorts [3] [7].

  • Open versus closed kinetic chain guidance

    • Inclusion of both Contemporary reviews recommend using both CKC and OKC exercises as complementary; some guidelines advocate early, controlled OKC quadriceps loading to restore strength while monitoring symptoms [5] [7].

  • Graft‑specific considerations

    • Allograft and graft biology Rehabilitation should account for graft incorporation timing and surgical specifics; allografts may require cautious progression given biologic incorporation differences, and clinicians should integrate surgeon guidance into progression decisions [8] [5].
    • Surgical variations Concomitant meniscal or chondral procedures and graft harvest site morbidity (eg, patellar tendon or hamstring) influence exercise selection and loading progression [2] [5].

  • Common rehabilitation mistakes and reinjury prevention

    • Frequent errors Premature return to high‑risk sport tasks, reliance on time only (not objective criteria), inadequate quadriceps or hip strength restoration, and neglecting neuromuscular/symmetry deficits [5] [7].
    • Prevention strategies Use criterion‑based progression, targeted neuromuscular training programs, and a post‑return secondary prevention program; RCT evidence supports specialized neuromuscular programs in reducing second‑ACL injury risk compared with strength‑only approaches [6] [5].

  • Novel and emerging approaches

    • Adjunct therapies Blood‑flow restriction and neuromuscular electrical stimulation are promising adjuncts under active investigation for accelerating strength gains and addressing quadriceps inhibition, but evidence is still evolving [9] [10].
    • Early‑stage optimization Recent work emphasizes prehabilitation and structured early‑stage strategies to maximize later outcomes and adherence to objective milestones [10] [3].

One-Minute-Paper Topics

A One-Minute-Paper (OMP) is a short, focused prompt that students answer in ~60 seconds at the end of a session to consolidate learning, surface misconceptions, and provide formative feedback. When answering, be concise, specific, and use terminology from today’s session.

OMP 1 — Goals of Early Phase Rehabilitation (0–6 weeks)

The primary goals are restoring pain-free range of motion, controlling swelling, re-establishing quadriceps activation, and normalising gait. Early weight bearing is widely recommended.

  • Why is early weight bearing after ACL reconstruction considered beneficial rather than harmful to graft recovery?
  • What are the three most important clinical signs that indicate an athlete is ready to progress from early to mid-phase rehabilitation?

OMP 2 — Quadriceps Inhibition in the Early Phase

Quadriceps inhibition is a common early post-operative complication. Neuromuscular electrical stimulation (NMES) is used as an adjunct when voluntary activation is insufficient.

  • What is the mechanism of arthrogenic muscle inhibition (AMI) after ACL reconstruction, and how does joint effusion contribute to it?
  • Under what conditions would you consider NMES as an adjunct for quadriceps activation, and what are its limitations?

OMP 3 — Role of Cryotherapy and Adjunct Modalities

Cryotherapy, compression, and short-term modalities may permit earlier pain-free exercise, but evidence for optimal dose-response is limited.

  • Given the limited dose-response evidence for cryotherapy, how should clinicians decide whether and how to use it in early ACL rehabilitation?
  • What is the difference between using a modality to “facilitate exercise participation” versus as a primary therapeutic intervention?

OMP 4 — Criterion-Based vs. Time-Based Progression

Protocols endorse progressing based on pain, effusion, and movement quality rather than calendar time alone. This “criterion-based” model is central to evidence-based ACL rehabilitation.

  • Why does calendar-based progression alone lead to suboptimal outcomes in ACL rehabilitation?
  • What objective criteria would you use to decide when an athlete can progress from heel slides and straight-leg raises to low-load closed-chain exercises?

OMP 5 — Mid-Phase Strength Progression (6–16 weeks)

The mid phase prioritises progressive resistance (bilateral then unilateral) and neuromuscular control. Running return protocols begin only when objective strength, ROM, and gait criteria are met.

  • What is the physiological rationale for progressing from bilateral to unilateral resistance exercises in the mid phase, and what movement quality deficits are targeted?
  • How do you determine when an athlete is ready to begin a running return protocol? What minimum strength and symmetry criteria should be met?

OMP 6 — Neuromuscular Training in the Mid Phase

Neuromuscular training (balance, single-leg stability, perturbation training, movement technique drills) improves outcomes and may reduce second-injury risk compared to strength-only approaches.

  • What specific neuromuscular deficits are most predictive of re-injury after ACL reconstruction, and how does targeted training address them?
  • How does perturbation training differ from standard balance training in terms of neurophysiological mechanisms and clinical outcomes?

OMP 7 — Posterior Chain and Hip Strengthening

Hip abduction and posterior chain strengthening (Romanian deadlifts, hip resistance band work) are emphasised alongside quadriceps training in the mid phase.

  • Why is isolated quadriceps strengthening insufficient for safe return to sport after ACL reconstruction?
  • What is the evidence linking hip weakness to ACL re-injury risk, particularly in female athletes?

OMP 8 — Open vs. Closed Kinetic Chain Exercises

Contemporary reviews recommend using both open (OKC) and closed kinetic chain (CKC) exercises as complementary. Controlled early OKC loading may restore quadriceps strength while monitoring symptoms.

  • What are the biomechanical differences between OKC and CKC exercises in terms of anterior tibial shear force and graft stress?
  • At what rehabilitation stage, and under what conditions, is early controlled OKC loading appropriate after ACL reconstruction?

OMP 9 — Late Phase Goals: Power, Plyometrics, and Agility (16+ weeks)

Late phase targets high-velocity strength, power, multidirectional plyometrics, and progressive exposure to cutting and pivoting under increasing speed and fatigue.

  • What is the rationale for the specific plyometric progression order: bilateral low-amplitude → single-leg vertical/horizontal → multidirectional/sport-specific?
  • How does fatigue exposure in late-phase training prepare the neuromuscular system for competition-level demands?

OMP 10 — Limb Symmetry Index (LSI) as a Return-to-Sport Criterion

An LSI of ≥90% for quadriceps strength and hop tests is the most commonly used threshold in criterion-based return-to-sport models.

  • What does a quadriceps LSI of 90% actually mean in practice, and why is this specific threshold so widely used?
  • What are the known limitations of LSI-based criteria — that is, what does passing LSI thresholds fail to guarantee?

OMP 11 — The Return-to-Sport Test Battery

Recommended battery components include isokinetic strength testing, multiple hop tests (single, triple, crossover, 6-m timed), movement quality assessment, and patient-reported outcomes.

  • Why is a multi-component test battery more predictive than any single test alone for return-to-sport clearance?
  • How would you adapt a full return-to-sport test battery for a setting with limited equipment (e.g., community sports clinic without isokinetic dynamometer)?

OMP 12 — Timing: Why 9–12 Months for Unrestricted Return?

Rehabilitation commonly extends to 9–12 months before unrestricted sport, with earlier return linked to higher re-injury risk in some cohorts.

  • What biological processes in graft maturation (ligamentisation) support the recommendation to delay unrestricted return to sport for 9–12 months?
  • An athlete passes all functional criteria at 7 months post-surgery. Should they be cleared for unrestricted competition? Justify your answer with evidence.

OMP 13 — Graft-Specific Considerations

Rehabilitation should account for graft type (patellar tendon, hamstring, allograft), harvest site morbidity, and biological incorporation timing.

  • How do patellar tendon and hamstring grafts differ in terms of rehabilitation implications, particularly for early quadriceps and posterior chain loading?
  • Why might allograft ACL reconstruction require more cautious rehabilitation progression than autograft procedures?

OMP 14 — Concomitant Meniscal and Chondral Procedures

Co-existing meniscal or chondral repairs influence exercise selection and loading progression during rehabilitation.

  • How does a simultaneous meniscal repair change the rehabilitation protocol compared to isolated ACL reconstruction?
  • What movement patterns or exercises should be modified or avoided in the presence of a medial meniscal repair during the first 12 weeks?

OMP 15 — Common Rehabilitation Mistakes

Frequent errors include premature return to high-risk activities, reliance on time-only criteria, inadequate quadriceps and hip strength restoration, and neglecting neuromuscular symmetry deficits.

  • Identify the single most dangerous error in ACL rehabilitation and explain the injury mechanism through which it leads to re-injury.
  • How would you design a quality control checklist to prevent the most common rehabilitation mistakes in a busy clinical setting?

OMP 16 — Secondary Prevention Programs

Specialised neuromuscular programs (e.g., the Delaware protocol) are supported by RCT evidence for reducing second ACL injury risk compared to strength-only approaches.

  • What is the evidence for neuromuscular training programmes in reducing second ACL injury, and how long should such programmes continue after return to sport?
  • Which components of a secondary prevention programme (strength, neuromuscular control, education, load management) do you consider most critical, and why?

OMP 17 — Blood Flow Restriction (BFR) Training as an Adjunct

BFR training is a promising adjunct under investigation for accelerating strength gains and addressing quadriceps inhibition, particularly in early rehabilitation phases.

  • What is the physiological mechanism by which low-load BFR training produces hypertrophy and strength gains comparable to high-load training?
  • In which specific phase(s) of ACL rehabilitation is BFR training most clinically justified, and what safety precautions apply?

OMP 18 — Prehabilitation: Optimising Pre-Operative Strength

Recent work emphasises prehabilitation (pre-operative strength and neuromuscular conditioning) to maximise post-operative outcomes.

  • What is the evidence that pre-operative quadriceps strength predicts post-operative rehabilitation outcomes after ACL reconstruction?
  • Design a 4-week prehabilitation protocol for an athlete awaiting ACL reconstruction surgery.

OMP 19 — Patient-Reported Outcomes in ACL Rehabilitation

Patient-reported outcome measures (PROMs) such as IKDC and KOS-ADLS capture subjective function and quality of life across rehabilitation phases.

  • What is the added value of PROMs compared to objective performance tests (strength, hop tests) in ACL rehabilitation assessment?
  • How should a clinician interpret a scenario where objective criteria are met (LSI ≥90%, hop tests passed) but the athlete reports persistently low PROM scores?

OMP 20 — Integrating the Full ACL Rehabilitation Framework

The evidence-based model integrates early motion and quadriceps activation, progressive neuromuscular and strength training, and criterion-based late-stage sport-specific reintegration across 9–12 months.

  • Summarise the key criterion-based milestones for advancing through each phase of ACL rehabilitation in a table or structured format.
  • What is the single most important change you would make to a standard institutional ACL rehabilitation protocol based on the evidence presented in this lecture?
