ACL Return To Competition Insights

July 3, 2026

Time versus criteria

Return decisions historically relied on fixed postoperative timelines, but contemporary evidence and expert groups advocate adding objective criteria and delaying clearance for high-risk athletes. This section contrasts the prevailing time-based practice with validated criterion-based approaches and common recommended minimum timelines.

  • Historical practice: Time dominant criteria (used alone) were common in the literature, and many studies allowed unrestricted RTS before 9 months postoperatively [1].
  • Criterion-based model: The Delaware protocol emphasizes resolving clinical impairments, ≥90% LSI on quadriceps strength and multiple single-leg hop tests, patient-reported outcomes, and an appropriate healing time (generally ≥9 months) before full competition [2].
  • Practical recommendations: Several contemporary reviews and guideline syntheses recommend combining time with objective milestones (eg, strength, hop tests, PROs) and delaying RTS for high-level or pediatric athletes; some authors propose minimums of 8–9 months as part of a multifactorial clearance [3] [4].

References: time prevalence and early RTS findings [1]; Delaware objective criteria and ≥9 months recommendation [2]; guideline-level recommendations for delayed RTS and strengthening testing [3] [4].

Functional tests and thresholds

Functional testing is central to criterion-based clearance; common batteries include quadriceps strength, multiple hop tests, and movement-quality assessments, with limb symmetry index thresholds frequently used as pass criteria. Psychological readiness and re-injury risk factors

Psychological status and specific patient and exposure factors influence return outcomes and reinjury risk; contemporary recommendations incorporate validated psychological scales and identify modifiable and nonmodifiable risk factors. The section outlines validated psychological measures and evidence-backed risk correlates.

  • Psychological measures: ACL‑RSI and other patient-reported readiness scales are recommended components of RTS batteries and are often used with numeric cutoffs in test protocols [5] [6].
  • Key modifiable risk factors: strength asymmetry (LSI <90%), poor movement quality/fatigue responses, and early return (<8–9 months) are associated with higher reinjury risk and are targets for remediation [2] [7] [8].
  • Key nonmodifiable or contextual risk factors: younger age and female sex have been identified as contributors to higher reinjury rates in several reviews and pooled analyses [7] [4].
  • Clinical synthesis: Authors recommend assessing psychological readiness alongside physical tests and including interventions (eg, sport psychology, graded exposure) when scores indicate fear or low confidence [5] [6].

Evidence: psychological test inclusion and thresholds in batteries [5]; younger age and earlier RTS as risk factors in pediatric/adolescent cohorts [7]; movement quality, fatigue, and chronic load considerations as reinjury contributors [8]; modifying factors such as age and sex noted in systematic syntheses [4].

Sport specific and consensus guidelines

Sport demands, staged reintegration, and several society-level recommendations guide tailoring RTS pathways; recent consensus and expert frameworks recommend multi-domain, sport-specific progressions rather than a one-size-fits-all rule. This section summarizes sport-specific progressions, professional guidance, and emerging organizational recommendations.

  • Sport-specific progressions: Progressive on-field and on-ice reintegration phases are advocated (eg, Delaware staged progression from individual drills to full competition; on-ice 4‑phase hockey progression) with objective criteria to enter each phase [2] [9].
  • Team-sport and high-demand sport frameworks: Frameworks for team sports emphasize retraining high-intensity repeated actions, cutting, decelerations, and reactive skills with motor-learning principles and graduated complexity [10] [11].
  • Consensus and guideline highlights: Multiple contemporary reviews and guideline papers call for objective, criterion-based RTS testing integrated with shared decision-making and a biopsychosocial model; professional recommendations commonly include strength testing, ≥2 dynamic tests, psychological screening, and a delayed timeline (commonly ≥8–9 months) as core components [12] [3] [4] [13].
  • Practical implementation: Use sport-specific drills, progressive exposure to contact/opposition, and monitoring of chronic training load during late-stage rehab to restore sport readiness and reduce reinjury risk [9] [10] [11].

Cited sources include sport-specific progressions and on-ice protocol [9], team-sport progressive frameworks [10], and consensus-style recommendations and reviews urging multifactorial RTS approaches [12] [3] [4] [13].

Emerging recommendations and gaps

Emerging approaches expand testing beyond traditional strength/hop batteries toward biomechanics, fatigue and load metrics, imaging, and longitudinal monitoring; validation of these methods is ongoing. This section lists novel directions and the principal evidence gaps to inform future practice.

  • Novel assessment domains: movement-quality biomechanical analysis, fatigue testing, and explosive neuromuscular performance are proposed additions to better capture sport demands and re-injury mechanisms [8] [11].
  • Imaging and biomarkers: MRI-based graft assessment and other imaging tools have been suggested as potential adjuncts to inform tissue healing and readiness, but predictive utility remains investigational [14].
  • Service-delivery and decision models: Recommendations favor a layered, continuous evaluation model with repeated athletic assessments through rehabilitation, shared decision-making, and individualized, sport-specific reintegration plans rather than a single end-point test [13] [15].
  • Evidence gaps: Current batteries (including ≥90% LSI rules) lack proven predictive validity for reinjury prevention; high-quality prospective studies that test whether meeting combined, sport-specific criteria reduces second-injury rates are still needed [1] [5] [16].

Key emerging proposals come from late-stage rehabilitation reviews and commentaries that advocate expanding testing scopes and validating new metrics in prospective cohorts [8] [11] [14] [13].

One-Minute-Paper Topics

A One-Minute-Paper (OMP) is a short, focused prompt that students answer in ~60 seconds at the end of a session to consolidate learning, surface misconceptions, and provide formative feedback. When answering, be concise, specific, and use terminology from today’s session.

OMP 1 — Time-Based vs. Criterion-Based Return-to-Sport (RTS)

Historically, fixed postoperative timelines dominated clinical practice. Contemporary evidence supports criterion-based approaches combined with time minimums (typically ≥8–9 months).

  • Why was time alone considered sufficient for return-to-sport decisions historically, and what evidence triggered the shift toward criterion-based models?
  • What are the risks for the athlete if they return before 8 months post-surgery, even if they meet all functional criteria at that point?

OMP 2 — The Delaware Protocol

The Delaware protocol requires resolution of clinical impairments, ≥90% LSI on quadriceps strength and multiple hop tests, patient-reported outcomes, and ≥9 months healing time.

  • What makes the Delaware protocol a “gold standard” reference in the return-to-sport literature?
  • What are the specific components of the Delaware protocol, and how does each domain contribute to the overall return-to-competition decision?

OMP 3 — Multifactorial RTS Decision-Making

Contemporary reviews recommend combining time with objective milestones (strength, hop tests, PROs) and delaying RTS for high-level or paediatric athletes.

  • Why should return-to-sport decisions in paediatric athletes be treated differently compared to adult athletes?
  • What stakeholder roles (athlete, clinician, coach, surgeon) contribute to a multifactorial shared decision-making model, and how should conflicts between them be managed?

OMP 4 — Quadriceps Strength as a Key RTS Criterion

A quadriceps LSI of ≥90% (or normalised torque benchmarks) is the most consistently cited criterion across test batteries and guideline documents.

  • What clinical method would you use to assess quadriceps strength in a setting without an isokinetic dynamometer?
  • Why is isolated quadriceps strength considered a key — but not sufficient — criterion for safe return to competition?

OMP 5 — Hop Tests: Battery, Thresholds, and Limitations

The standard hop test battery (single hop, triple hop, crossover hop, 6-m timed hop) requires ≥90% LSI on all tests. However, passing hop tests does not guarantee protection from re-injury.

  • What physical qualities do hop tests actually measure, and what qualities relevant to competition do they fail to capture?
  • How would you implement and score a full four-test hop battery in a clinical setting? What are the key standardisation requirements?

OMP 6 — Movement Quality Assessment (LESS)

The Landing Error Scoring System (LESS) and qualitative movement analysis are recommended as adjuncts, with LESS ≤5 used as a threshold in some protocols.

  • What landing mechanics errors does the LESS assess, and which are most strongly associated with ACL re-injury risk?
  • Why is visual movement quality assessment considered an essential complement to quantitative strength and hop testing?

OMP 7 — Patient-Reported Outcomes in RTS Clearance

PROMs such as IKDC, KOS-ADLS (≥90% threshold), and ACL-RSI cutoffs are increasingly included alongside physical tests as mandatory components of RTS batteries.

  • What aspects of athlete readiness do PROMs capture that cannot be measured by physical performance tests?
  • How should a clinician respond when an athlete meets all physical criteria but scores below the KOS-ADLS threshold?

OMP 8 — Psychological Readiness: ACL-RSI

The ACL-RSI and other patient-reported psychological readiness scales assess emotions, confidence, and risk appraisal. Numeric cutoffs are used in some test protocols.

  • What are the main psychological barriers to return to sport after ACL reconstruction, and how does the ACL-RSI capture them?
  • What interventions (sport psychology, graded exposure, etc.) have evidence for improving ACL-RSI scores in athletes who score below readiness thresholds?

OMP 9 — Fear of Reinjury as a Re-Injury Risk Factor

Low psychological readiness and fear of reinjury are associated with higher re-injury rates, independent of physical functional criteria.

  • What is the neurophysiological mechanism through which fear of reinjury may increase biomechanical movement deficits and re-injury risk?
  • Should clearance for return to competition be withheld purely on the basis of low psychological readiness scores? Justify your position.

OMP 10 — Age and Sex as Risk Factors

Younger age and female sex are identified as contributors to higher reinjury rates in multiple reviews and pooled analyses.

  • What anatomical, neuromuscular, and hormonal factors contribute to the higher ACL re-injury risk in younger and female athletes?
  • How should return-to-sport protocols be modified for adolescent female athletes to account for these elevated risk profiles?

OMP 11 — Strength Asymmetry (LSI <90%) as a Modifiable Risk Factor

Persistent quadriceps strength asymmetry below 90% LSI is one of the strongest modifiable predictors of ACL re-injury and is an explicit target for remediation.

  • What practical strategies are most effective for reducing persistent quadriceps asymmetry (LSI <90%) in the late rehabilitation phase?
  • At what point should a clinician consider that residual strength asymmetry represents a permanent deficit rather than a rehabilitation gap?

OMP 12 — Multidomain Test Battery: Passing Criteria

Studies consistently recommend passing strength, ≥2 dynamic tests, psychological readiness, and clinical exam as a core combined clearance framework. Passing all domains reduces outcome heterogeneity.

  • Why does the combination of multiple domains (strength + dynamic tests + PROs) reduce outcome heterogeneity better than any single domain alone?
  • What is the minimum number of test domains that should be passed to constitute a defensible return-to-competition clearance decision?

OMP 13 — Sport-Specific Progressions

The Delaware staged progression and on-ice 4-phase hockey progressions illustrate how late-stage rehabilitation must incorporate sport-specific tasks before full competition clearance.

  • Why is generalised rehabilitation — without sport-specific movement reintegration — insufficient for safe return to team sport competition?
  • Design a 4-week sport-specific reintegration programme for a football (soccer) player preparing to return after ACL reconstruction.

OMP 14 — Team Sport Frameworks: High-Intensity Repeated Actions

Team sport frameworks emphasise retraining cutting, deceleration, reactive skills, and high-intensity repeated actions with motor-learning principles and graduated complexity.

  • What is the role of reactive agility training in late-phase ACL rehabilitation, and how does it differ from pre-planned agility drills?
  • How does chronic training load management during late-stage rehabilitation influence re-injury risk in team sport athletes?

OMP 15 — Biopsychosocial Model in RTS Decision-Making

Multiple consensus guidelines call for integrating objective testing with shared decision-making and a biopsychosocial model for return-to-sport clearance.

  • How does the biopsychosocial model differ from a purely biomedical approach to return-to-sport decision-making?
  • What specific psychosocial factors beyond ACL-RSI should be assessed in a comprehensive return-to-competition evaluation?

OMP 16 — Biomechanical Analysis as an Emerging Assessment Domain

Movement-quality biomechanical analysis, fatigue testing, and explosive neuromuscular performance are proposed extensions beyond traditional strength and hop batteries.

  • What biomechanical measures (e.g., knee valgus angle, ground reaction forces) are most sensitive to residual deficits after ACL reconstruction and associated with re-injury?
  • What practical barriers prevent routine biomechanical gait analysis from being included in standard clinical return-to-sport batteries?

OMP 17 — Fatigue Testing in RTS Assessment

Fatigue-induced alterations in landing mechanics and neuromuscular control are proposed as additional assessment targets, particularly given that competition injuries often occur under fatigue.

  • Why does testing neuromuscular control under fatigue better predict competition risk than non-fatigued testing alone?
  • Design a simple fatigue protocol that could be incorporated into a routine return-to-sport test battery without specialised laboratory equipment.

OMP 18 — MRI-Based Graft Assessment

MRI imaging has been proposed as a potential adjunct to assess graft maturation and tissue healing, but predictive utility for return-to-sport clearance remains investigational.

  • What does MRI-based graft assessment currently reveal about the status of ACL graft maturation, and what can it not yet reliably predict?
  • At what timepoint post-surgery and in which clinical scenarios would MRI graft assessment provide the most useful information for return-to-competition decisions?

OMP 19 — Predictive Validity of Current Criteria: The Evidence Gap

Current batteries (including the ≥90% LSI rule) lack proven predictive validity for re-injury prevention. Prospective studies testing combined sport-specific criteria are still needed.

  • What type of study design would be required to definitively establish whether meeting combined criterion-based RTS criteria reduces second-injury rates?
  • Given the acknowledged limitations of current criteria, how should clinicians communicate uncertainty about return-to-competition decisions to athletes and coaches?

OMP 20 — Layered, Continuous Evaluation Model

A continuous, layered evaluation model with repeated assessments through rehabilitation, shared decision-making, and individualised sport-specific reintegration is preferred over a single endpoint test.

  • What are the advantages of a longitudinal, repeated assessment model over a single “final clearance” test for return-to-competition decisions?
  • How would you implement a practical longitudinal monitoring programme for an ACL-reconstructed athlete from surgery to full competition return?
