RTC Test Battery – Longitudinal Comparison and Clinical Interpretation

July 10, 2026

Overview

This lecture presents a longitudinal case study of athlete xyz18 (KS224), a 22-year-old male soccer player who sustained a right ankle injury on 31.08.2024. Two Return to Competition (RTC) test battery assessments were performed at different rehabilitation stages and are compared systematically to illustrate clinical interpretation, threshold-based classification, and evidence-based decision-making for return-to-sport clearance.

The RTC test battery comprises functional hop tests, dynamic balance assessments, self-reported questionnaires for ankle instability, psychological readiness, and pain, providing a multidimensional assessment framework for return-to-sport decisions.

1. Hop Tests and Functional Symmetry

The Limb Symmetry Index (LSI) is calculated as the ratio of the weaker to the stronger side (multiplied by 100%). LSI thresholds classify injury risk as follows: 90–110% is considered normal (green), 85–89% or 111–115% indicates slightly increased risk (yellow), and values below 85% or above 115% indicate significantly increased risk (red).

  • Test 1 findings: All five hop tests (Single, Triple, Triple-X, Square, Side) showed marked asymmetries with LSI values ranging from 76.4% to 81.8%, well below the 90% clinical threshold. The injured right side demonstrated significantly reduced jump distances across all tests, consistent with the expected profile at 76 days post-injury.

  • Test 2 findings: All LSI values improved to the range of 92.4–100.0%, placing them within the green normal range. Symmetry improved by 14–24 percentage points. This meets the minimum criterion for return-to-sport clearance as described by Grindem et al. 2016 and van Melick et al. 2016.

  • Double-Hop tests: Bilateral jump performance (single and triple hop) also showed marked improvement from Test 1 to Test 2 in absolute jump distances, reflecting improved bilateral power generation.

2. Y-Balance Test (YBT) – Dynamic Balance

The Y-Balance Test assesses dynamic single-limb balance in three reach directions: Anterior, Posteromedial, and Posterolateral. Clinical thresholds for side-to-side difference are: less than 2 cm indicates good balance (green), 2–4 cm indicates noticeable imbalance (yellow), and greater than 4 cm indicates increased injury risk (red). Plisky et al. (2006) demonstrated that anterior side differences greater than 4 cm are associated with a 2.5-fold increased injury risk.

  • Test 1 findings: Massive side-to-side differences in all three directions (8–14 cm), all well above the 4 cm threshold. The injured right side showed substantially reduced reach distances, indicating significant proprioceptive and neuromuscular deficits.

  • Test 2 findings: Side-to-side differences reduced to 0.0–1.7 cm, all within the green range. The proprioceptive and neuromuscular control of the injured side has largely normalized.

3. Star Excursion Balance Test (SEBT)

The SEBT extends the balance assessment to four directions: Anterior, Medial Dexter, Medial Sinister, and Posterior. The same clinical thresholds apply as for the YBT (2 cm and 4 cm side-to-side difference cutoffs).

  • Test 1 findings: Extreme side-to-side differences (13–18 cm), particularly in the posterior and medial directions, indicating severe balance and neuromuscular control deficits on the injured side.

  • Test 2 findings: Differences reduced to 0.3–1.7 cm. The balance profile is now largely symmetric, demonstrating effective rehabilitation of neuromuscular control.

4. CAIT – Cumberland Ankle Instability Tool

The CAIT is a self-reported questionnaire (maximum 30 points) that quantifies perceived ankle instability. Scores of 24 or below indicate chronic ankle instability (red), scores of 25–27 indicate increased likelihood of instability (yellow), and scores above 27 are unremarkable (green). Hiller et al. (2006) established the CAIT with an ICC of 0.96. Wright et al. (2014) recalibrated the cutoff to 25 or below.

  • Test 1 findings: CAIT right (injured) scored 13 points, indicating severe chronic instability. CAIT left scored 22 points, also below the instability threshold.

  • Test 2 findings: CAIT right improved to 27 points (improvement of 14 points). CAIT left remained at 22 points.

  • Recommendation: Continue targeted proprioceptive and sensorimotor training for ankle stability. The injured side has improved substantially but should be monitored.

5. I-PRRS – Psychological Readiness to Return to Sport

The Injury-Psychological Readiness to Return to Sport (I-PRRS) scale measures psychological readiness across 6 items, each scored 0–100: Confidence in Performance, Confidence in Competition, Relaxation in Competition, No Fear of Reinjury, Confidence in Sport Skill, and Confidence Giving 100%. A mean score below 50 indicates the athlete is psychologically not yet ready to return to sport. According to Glazer (2009), a score above 60 indicates maximum confidence.

  • Test 1 findings: I-PRRS mean of 35.0, well below the threshold of 50. The athlete was psychologically not ready to return to sport. Particularly low scores on No Fear of Reinjury (25) and Relaxation in Competition (30) reflect significant psychological barriers.

  • Test 2 findings: I-PRRS mean improved dramatically to 95.8, confirming psychological readiness. All items scored 75 or above. The improvement of 60.8 points represents one of the largest changes across the entire test battery.

6. MIS – Manchester Instability Score

The MIS captures pain symptoms across 5 subscales: Pressure, Stretch Pain, Neutral Position, End Feel, and Knee Extension. Higher scores indicate more pain and instability. A total score of 3 or below is considered normal, 4–8 warrants observation, and above 8 is abnormal.

  • Test 1 findings: MIS total of 25 points, indicating significant pain symptoms. Highest subscale scores were Stretch Pain (7), Pressure (6), and End Feel (5).

  • Test 2 findings: MIS total reduced to 0, the athlete is completely pain-free. This reduction of 25 points is a strong positive indicator for the return-to-sport decision.

7. Normalized Balance Reach Distances

YBT and SEBT reach distances are normalized to leg length (measured as left 86.5 cm, right 86.3 cm) and expressed as a percentage. This normalization allows comparison across athletes of different body dimensions and is recommended in the literature. A reference value of 94% leg length has been proposed as a benchmark for adequate dynamic balance.

  • Test 1: Normalized values showed substantial asymmetry with the injured right side consistently below both the uninjured side and the 94% reference across all reach directions.

  • Test 2: Normalized values are largely symmetric and near or above the 94% reference for most directions, indicating adequate functional balance capacity.

8. Clinical Traffic Light Classification

Each test domain is classified using a traffic light system (green, yellow, red) based on established clinical thresholds. This provides a rapid visual summary of the athlete’s readiness across all domains.

9. Evidence-Based Interpretation

Test 1 – Early Rehabilitation Phase (76 days post-injury)

The initial assessment presents the expected pattern of an early rehabilitation phase after right ankle injury: significant hop test asymmetries (LSI 76–82%), massive balance deficits (side-to-side differences of 8–18 cm), severe perceived ankle instability (CAIT 13), low psychological readiness (I-PRRS 35.0), and substantial pain symptoms (MIS 25). All 16 domains are classified as red.

Test 2 – Late Rehabilitation Phase (166 days post-injury)

The follow-up assessment demonstrates marked improvement across nearly all domains: hop test symmetry restored to 92–100% (all green), balance differences reduced to 0–2 cm (predominantly green), dramatic improvement in psychological readiness (I-PRRS 95.8), and complete pain resolution (MIS 0). Only CAIT on the injured side remains a concern.

One-Minute-Paper Topics

A One-Minute-Paper (OMP) is a short, focused prompt that students answer in ~60 seconds at the end of a session to consolidate learning, surface misconceptions, and provide formative feedback. When answering, be concise, specific, and use terminology from today’s session.

OMP 1 — Longitudinal Case Study Design

Athlete KS224, a 22-year-old male soccer player with a right ankle injury, was assessed at two timepoints: 76 days and 166 days post-injury.

  • What are the clinical advantages of longitudinal (repeated) assessment using a standardised test battery compared to a single endpoint assessment?
  • What additional contextual information (training history, surgical details, comorbidities) would you want before interpreting the two test batteries?

OMP 2 — Limb Symmetry Index (LSI): Concept and Thresholds

LSI = (weaker side / stronger side) × 100%. Traffic light classification: ≥90–110% = green; 85–89%/111–115% = yellow; <85%/>115% = red.

  • Why is the LSI calculated relative to the contralateral limb rather than to population normative values?
  • What potential misclassification would occur if the contralateral “healthy” limb was itself weakened (e.g., due to compensatory disuse)?

OMP 3 — Hop Test Findings: Test 1 vs. Test 2

At 76 days: LSI 76–82% (all red). At 166 days: LSI 92–100% (all green), with 14–24 percentage point improvements per test.

  • What do the specific LSI values at 76 days (76–82%) tell you about the expected recovery trajectory for a post-ankle-injury athlete?
  • How would you use the hop test improvement data to counsel the athlete and coach about training load progression after Test 2?

OMP 4 — Double-Hop Tests: Bilateral Power Generation

Bilateral absolute jump distances also improved markedly between Test 1 and Test 2, reflecting improved bilateral power generation beyond simple symmetry.

  • Why is absolute bilateral performance (not just symmetry) clinically relevant in the context of return to competition?
  • Can an athlete have good LSI (≥90%) but still show clinically relevant absolute performance deficits? What would this mean?

OMP 5 — Y-Balance Test (YBT): Dynamic Single-Limb Balance

Clinical thresholds: <2 cm side difference = green; 2–4 cm = yellow; >4 cm = red. Anterior differences >4 cm are associated with a 2.5-fold increased injury risk (Plisky et al. 2006).

  • What neuromuscular qualities does the YBT assess, and why are proprioceptive deficits particularly relevant after ankle injury?
  • The athlete progressed from 8–14 cm side differences at Test 1 to 0–1.7 cm at Test 2. What rehabilitation modalities most likely contributed to this dramatic improvement?

OMP 6 — Star Excursion Balance Test (SEBT): Multidirectional Balance

SEBT assessed four directions (Anterior, Medial Dexter, Medial Sinister, Posterior). Test 1 showed 13–18 cm differences; Test 2 showed 0.3–1.7 cm.

  • Why are the posterior and medial directions of the SEBT particularly sensitive to ankle injury-related deficits?
  • The SEBT showed extreme deficits (up to 18 cm) at 76 days. What specific neuromuscular mechanisms explain such large bilateral differences at this stage of recovery?

OMP 7 — Cumberland Ankle Instability Tool (CAIT): Scoring and Interpretation

CAIT maximum = 30 points. ≤24 = chronic instability (red); 25–27 = increased likelihood (yellow); >27 = unremarkable (green). ICC 0.96 (Hiller et al. 2006).

  • What does an ICC of 0.96 for the CAIT mean clinically, and why is high reliability important for a return-to-sport questionnaire?
  • The athlete’s uninjured left ankle also scored below the instability threshold at both time points (22 points). What are the clinical implications of this finding?

OMP 8 — CAIT: Injured vs. Uninjured Side

CAIT right improved from 13 (severe instability) to 27 (just within yellow/green boundary). CAIT left remained at 22 (yellow) at both time points.

  • What does bilateral ankle instability (both sides below the CAIT threshold) suggest about this athlete’s general proprioceptive profile?
  • Should chronic ankle instability on the uninjured side influence the return-to-competition decision? What additional assessment would you recommend?

OMP 9 — I-PRRS: Psychological Readiness Assessment

I-PRRS mean scores: Test 1 = 35.0 (not ready); Test 2 = 95.8 (fully ready). The most dramatic improvement was in “No Fear of Reinjury” (from 25 to 90+).

  • Why did “No Fear of Reinjury” and “Relaxation in Competition” show the lowest I-PRRS subscale scores at Test 1, and what does this tell us about the athlete’s psychological barriers?
  • How would you systematically address low I-PRRS scores during rehabilitation — what psychological interventions are evidence-based?

OMP 10 — Magnitude of Psychological Change: 60.8 Points on I-PRRS

The 60.8-point improvement in I-PRRS was described as “one of the largest changes across the entire test battery.”

  • What does the magnitude of psychological change (60.8 points) relative to functional change tell us about the importance of psychological rehabilitation after ankle injury?
  • At what point in the rehabilitation timeline should psychological readiness first be formally assessed, and how frequently should it be re-evaluated?

OMP 11 — Manchester Instability Score (MIS): Pain Symptom Assessment

MIS maximum ≈ 25 (or above). Score ≤3 = normal; 4–8 = observation; >8 = abnormal. Test 1: 25 points; Test 2: 0 points (complete pain resolution).

  • What does a complete reduction in MIS score (from 25 to 0) between Tests 1 and 2 indicate about the athlete’s tissue healing trajectory?
  • How does pain-free status on the MIS contribute to the overall return-to-competition decision beyond what functional tests reveal?

OMP 12 — Normalised Balance Reach Distances

YBT and SEBT reach distances are normalised to leg length (%) to allow cross-athlete comparison. A reference value of 94% leg length is proposed as a benchmark.

  • Why is normalization to leg length important when comparing YBT/SEBT values across athletes of different body dimensions?
  • The 94% leg length benchmark — what population was this derived from, and how universally applicable is it to all athletic populations?

OMP 13 — Traffic Light Classification System

Test 1: 16/16 domains red, 0 green. Test 2: 14/16 domains green, 1 yellow, 1 red.

  • What are the advantages of a traffic light classification system for communicating test battery results to athletes, coaches, and non-specialist staff?
  • What clinical decision would you make for the remaining non-green domains at Test 2 — is the athlete cleared for competition? What conditions apply?

OMP 14 — Early vs. Late Rehabilitation Phase: Profile Interpretation

Test 1 presents the expected early-phase profile; Test 2 demonstrates late-phase multi-domain recovery. The interpretation framework uses phase-specific expectations.

  • Why is it clinically important to interpret individual test results in the context of the expected rehabilitation phase, rather than applying fixed thresholds without context?
  • What would an unexpected finding at Test 1 (e.g., unexpectedly good LSI) tell you about the quality of the clinical picture?

OMP 15 — Shared Decision-Making Principle (Ardern et al. 2016)

The overall assessment recommends following the Shared Decision-Making principle for the return-to-sport decision.

  • What does Shared Decision-Making mean in a sports medicine context, and who are the key stakeholders?
  • How would you structure a Shared Decision-Making conversation with athlete KS224 after Test 2 — what information would you present, and what would you ask?

OMP 16 — Continued Proprioceptive Training Recommendation

Despite meeting most criteria at Test 2, continued proprioceptive and sensorimotor training for ankle stability was recommended due to the CAIT finding.

  • Why does a single persistent deficit (CAIT) justify a continued training recommendation even when all other domains are in the green range?
  • Design a 4-week targeted proprioceptive training programme for ankle stability after return to sport.

OMP 17 — Multidimensional Assessment Framework

The RTC battery combines hop tests (functional symmetry), balance tests (neuromuscular control), questionnaires (instability, pain, psychological readiness), and provides a unified clinical picture.

  • What specific clinical decision would be different if the assessment had relied only on hop tests (LSI) without the balance and psychological components?
  • Which domain of the RTC battery do you consider the most undervalued in everyday clinical practice, and why?

OMP 18 — Inter-Domain Coherence and Clinical Red Flags

A consistent finding across all domains at Test 1 (all red) and Test 2 (mostly green) indicates coherent recovery. Inconsistencies between domains would be clinically significant.

  • What would it mean clinically if hop test LSI was in the green range but YBT differences remained >4 cm at Test 2?
  • What domain combinations at Test 2 would constitute an absolute contraindication to return to competition?

OMP 19 — Test Battery in Soccer-Specific Context

The athlete is a soccer player, and the RTC battery must ultimately be interpreted in the context of soccer-specific functional demands.

  • How well does the standard RTC battery assess readiness for the specific demands of soccer (e.g., high-intensity sprinting, cutting, aerial duels)?
  • What soccer-specific tests or movement assessments would you add to the standard battery to improve return-to-competition decision accuracy?

OMP 20 — Principles of Evidence-Based Return-to-Competition Decision-Making

The lecture integrates validated instruments, established thresholds, and multi-domain assessment into a systematic, reproducible clinical framework.

  • Summarise the key principles that distinguish evidence-based return-to-competition decision-making from intuitive or experience-based clinical judgment.
  • If you were designing an improved version of the RTC test battery for ankle injuries, what modifications would you make based on the evidence and case study presented?
