## One-Minute-Paper Topics
<Note>
  A **One-Minute-Paper** (OMP) is a short, focused prompt that students answer in ~60 seconds at the end of a session to consolidate learning, surface misconceptions, and provide formative feedback.  When answering, be concise, specific, and use terminology from today's session.
</Note>

### OMP 1 — Applying the Traffic Light System Across Different Cases
The traffic light classification (green/yellow/red) is the core clinical communication tool of the RTC battery. Applying it across different athletes and injury types reveals its versatility and limitations.

- What are the potential misclassification risks when applying the same LSI and balance thresholds across athletes from different sports (e.g., soccer vs. gymnastics)?
- How would you communicate a "yellow" classification to an athlete who is eager to return to competition?

---

### OMP 2 — Between-Case Variability in Recovery Profiles
Different athletes with the same injury and timepoint can show vastly different test battery profiles, reflecting individual differences in healing, training, and psychological readiness.

- What individual biological and training-related factors most strongly predict between-athlete variability in RTC battery performance at a given timepoint post-injury?
- How does acknowledging individual variability affect how you set expectations with athletes and coaches during rehabilitation?

---

### OMP 3 — Interpreting Isolated Domain Deficits
A common clinical challenge is interpreting cases where one domain remains below threshold while all others pass.

- Using the RTC framework, how would you manage a case where all hop tests pass (LSI ≥90%) but the athlete shows persistent balance deficits (YBT >4 cm asymmetry)?
- Should isolated psychological unreadiness (low I-PRRS) prevent return to competition when all physical criteria are met? Construct an argument for both positions.

---

### OMP 4 — Comparing Early vs. Late Rehabilitation Examples
Examples from early and late rehabilitation phases illustrate the expected trajectory of RTC battery improvements and help calibrate clinical expectations.

- What is the expected time course of normalisation for each domain (hop tests, balance, CAIT, I-PRRS, MIS) based on the evidence, and how do these trajectories differ?
- A clinician sees an athlete at 90 days post-ankle sprain with all hop tests in the yellow range. Is this expected or concerning? What action follows?

---

### OMP 5 — LSI Interpretation in Examples with Bilateral Injury History
When both limbs have been previously injured, the reference limb for LSI calculation may itself show deficits, complicating interpretation.

- How should LSI be calculated and interpreted in an athlete with a history of bilateral ankle injuries?
- What normative database approach (rather than contralateral limb comparison) could be used when bilateral injury history compromises LSI validity?

---

### OMP 6 — Balance Test Examples: Sport-Specific Expectations
Dynamic balance demands vary substantially across sports. Return-to-competition thresholds that are appropriate for a soccer player may be insufficient for a gymnast or basketball player.

- For which sports would you argue that the standard YBT/SEBT thresholds (&lt;2 cm asymmetry) are inadequate, and what sport-specific norms would you apply instead?
- How would you modify balance test protocols to better capture sport-specific dynamic balance demands beyond the standard YBT/SEBT directions?

---

### OMP 7 — CAIT Case Examples: Subclinical vs. Clinical Instability
CAIT scores across the spectrum (13, 22, 27, 30) represent different clinical states requiring different management strategies.

- Describe the clinical management for each of the following CAIT scenarios: (a) Score 13 — both sides; (b) Score 25 — injured side only; (c) Score 30 — both sides.
- What additional tests (clinical examination, imaging, functional tests) would you use to supplement the CAIT in a case with a low score on the uninjured side?

---

### OMP 8 — Psychological Readiness Examples: Range of I-PRRS Profiles
Across example cases, I-PRRS scores can range from &lt;30 to >90 at comparable timepoints. This variability has direct clinical implications.

- What case characteristics (injury severity, previous injury history, sport level, social support) are associated with persistently low I-PRRS scores despite physical recovery?
- In a case where I-PRRS scores stagnate below 50 for more than 8 weeks, what clinical pathway would you follow?

---

### OMP 9 — MIS Interpretation Across Example Cases
Zero MIS scores (complete pain-freedom) contrast with persistent pain profiles that may indicate residual pathology rather than simply incomplete rehabilitation.

- When MIS pain subscales remain elevated at 150+ days post-injury, what differential diagnoses should be considered (beyond rehabilitation deficits)?
- How would you modify the return-to-competition decision in a case with a normalised functional profile but persistent MIS pain subscale scores >3?

---

### OMP 10 — Normalised Balance Reach Distances: Cross-Case Interpretation
Normalising to leg length allows comparison across cases. The 94% benchmark as a reference value performs differently across sex, sport, and training background.

- Across the example cases, what patient characteristics most influence normalised YBT/SEBT reach distances independently of injury status?
- How would you establish sport- and sex-specific normative data for normalised balance reach distances in your own institutional setting?

---

### OMP 11 — Test Battery Administration: Standardisation Challenges
Consistent administration of the RTC battery across time points and clinicians requires strict standardisation of instructions, warm-up, order of tests, and scoring.

- What specific standardisation procedures (warm-up, instructions, trial attempts, rest periods) are most critical for reliable hop test performance?
- How would inconsistent test administration between Test 1 and Test 2 compromise the clinical validity of longitudinal comparison?

---

### OMP 12 — Error Analysis: Common Misinterpretation of RTC Data
Clinical examples reveal how RTC battery data can be misinterpreted, leading to premature clearance or unnecessarily delayed return to competition.

- Describe two specific misinterpretation errors that could lead to premature return-to-competition clearance, and how each could be avoided.
- Describe two specific misinterpretation errors that could lead to unnecessarily prolonged time away from competition, and what the athlete-level consequences might be.

---

### OMP 13 — Documentation of RTC Battery Results
Systematic documentation of test scores, thresholds, and clinical conclusions across multiple assessments supports continuity of care and transparent decision-making.

- What minimum documentation standard would you set for RTC battery assessments in a professional football academy?
- How could electronic health records or digital platforms improve the quality and accessibility of longitudinal RTC battery documentation?

---

### OMP 14 — Communication of Test Results to Non-Clinical Stakeholders
Coaches, athletic directors, and athletes themselves require RTC results to be communicated clearly, accurately, and without overstatement.

- How would you translate a nuanced test battery result (e.g., 14/16 green, 1 yellow, 1 red) into a clear recommendation for a head coach who does not have a sports medicine background?
- What are the ethical responsibilities of the sports medicine clinician when communicating return-to-competition readiness to performance-focused stakeholders?

---

### OMP 15 — Repeated Testing Frequency and Clinical Decision Points
How frequently should the RTC battery be repeated during rehabilitation, and what triggers a formal reassessment?

- What criteria should determine the scheduling of formal RTC battery reassessments during rehabilitation (time-based, milestone-based, or symptom-triggered)?
- Is there a minimum interval between RTC battery assessments below which test-retest reliability is compromised? What does the evidence suggest?

---

### OMP 16 — Return After Re-Injury: Modified Assessment Approach
When an athlete sustains a re-injury and undergoes a second RTC battery process, the clinical context differs from primary injury rehabilitation.

- How should the clinical thresholds and expectations be modified for an athlete undergoing their second RTC battery process after re-injury?
- What additional domains (not in the standard battery) would you include in an RTC assessment after re-injury to capture modifiable risk factors?

---

### OMP 17 — Sport-Level Considerations in RTC Battery Interpretation
Elite athletes may require higher performance benchmarks than the standard thresholds to safely resume competition at their specific level.

- Should elite athletes be held to higher RTC battery standards than recreational athletes? What evidence supports or contradicts this position?
- How would you modify the RTC battery protocol for a professional athlete in the final 4 weeks before a major competition?

---

### OMP 18 — Integration of Load Monitoring with RTC Decisions
Return-to-competition decisions should be contextualised within the athlete's current training load and acute:chronic workload ratio.

- Why is training load data (e.g., session-RPE, GPS data) a necessary contextual layer for interpreting RTC battery results?
- Design a monitoring framework that integrates RTC battery results with weekly training load data to guide return-to-full-training progression.

---

### OMP 19 — The Clinician's Role in Shaping Evidence-Based Practice
Clinicians who systematically collect and document RTC battery data contribute to the evidence base for optimal return-to-sport thresholds.

- How can routine clinical data collection (e.g., in a sports medicine practice) contribute to the development and validation of RTC battery thresholds?
- What study design would best address the current evidence gap regarding which RTC battery combinations most reliably predict safe return to competition?

---

### OMP 20 — Synthesis: Principles of Systematic RTC Battery Use
The RTC battery examples, when viewed collectively, illustrate core principles: multidimensional assessment, threshold-based classification, longitudinal tracking, and individualised clinical interpretation.

- What are the five most important lessons from the RTC battery example cases for evidence-based clinical practice in sports medicine?
- Reflecting on all RTC-related content across Lectures 6 and 7: what single change would most improve the clinical value of the current standard battery?
